## What a T-money Card Actually Does

T-money is Korea's rechargeable transit card. In practice, it is the easiest way to pay for public transport in Seoul, Busan, and most other major cities.

You tap in when you enter a subway gate or board a bus, and tap out when you leave. The fare is deducted automatically. You do not need to figure out zones, machine menus, or exact change every time you travel.

For first-time visitors, that convenience matters more than the small fare difference. The biggest mistake new travelers make is assuming they can just buy a paper ticket each time and that it will be equally easy. It is not. A T-money card removes friction from your trip from the first hour.

## Where to Buy a T-money Card

The easiest places to buy one are:

- Convenience stores at the airport such as CU, GS25, or 7-Eleven
- Convenience stores near subway stations
- Some station vending machines

If you land at Incheon Airport, buy it before you leave the arrivals area. That way you can use it immediately for the AREX All-Stop train, subway transfers, or local buses. If you are not sure how to get into the city, read our [ICN Airport to Seoul](/seoul/icn-airport-to-seoul/) guide first.

## How Much Money to Load First

For most first-time visitors, this is a practical starting point:

| Trip Length | Recommended First Top-Up | Why |
|---|---|---|
| 1-2 days | 15,000 KRW | Enough for airport + a few city rides |
| 3-5 days | 20,000-30,000 KRW | Comfortable buffer for subway and buses |
| 6-10 days | 30,000-45,000 KRW | Better if you move around a lot each day |

You do not need to overthink this. Topping up is easy, so the goal is simply to avoid running out on day one.

## When T-money Is Worth It

For almost every Seoul or Busan trip, it is worth it if:

- You are taking the subway from the airport into the city
- You expect to use buses even once or twice
- You are moving between neighborhoods each day
- You want the least confusing transport setup possible

It becomes especially useful in a city like Seoul, where you may stay in one area and sightsee in three others. For example, if you stay in [Myeongdong or Hongdae](/seoul/where-to-stay/), you will almost certainly use the subway several times a day.

## When You Might Skip It

You can reasonably skip a T-money card if all of these are true:

- Your trip is extremely short
- You are using private airport transfer
- Most of your movement is by taxi or on foot
- You do not plan to use buses

Even then, many travelers still buy one because the cost of the card is small compared with the convenience.

## Practical Things Visitors Usually Want to Know

### Can you use it on buses?

Yes. In fact, that is one of the main reasons to get it. Buses are harder to navigate if you need exact cash or separate payment every time.

### Can you use it outside Seoul?

Usually yes in major cities, including Busan. If your trip includes both Seoul and Busan, one card is much easier than learning a new payment method in each place.

### Do you need cash to top it up?

Often yes at convenience stores or machines, depending on the location. Carrying a little cash is useful for this reason alone.

### Can you get a refund?

Policies vary by card type and location, but small remaining balances can often be refunded with a fee. For most short-term visitors, it is simpler to load a sensible amount and use it up naturally before departure.

## Our Real Recommendation

If this is your first Korea trip, buy a T-money card on arrival and load enough for your first few days. It is one of the highest-convenience, lowest-effort purchases you can make. It will not transform your trip, but it will quietly remove dozens of small annoyances.

That is the kind of thing good travel prep should do.
