## eSIM — Best for Most Travelers

An eSIM is a digital SIM card. You buy a data plan online, scan a QR code, and your phone connects to a Korean network the moment you land. There is no physical card to swap, no device to pick up, and no return process.

**How it works:**

1. Buy an eSIM plan online before your flight (Airalo, Holafly, and eSIM2Fly are popular providers)
2. Scan the QR code or install via the provider's app
3. Activate the eSIM when you land at ICN
4. You are online immediately — use maps, messaging, and ride-hailing apps right away

**Cost:** Most 7-day plans cost $10–25 depending on data volume. Unlimited data plans are available for $20–30.

**What you get:** Data only in most cases. You will not get a Korean phone number, so you cannot receive local SMS or calls. For most visitors, this does not matter — you can use messaging apps for everything.

**Compatibility:** Your phone must support eSIM. Most iPhones from iPhone XS onward and most Samsung Galaxy phones from the S20 onward support it. Check your phone's settings before you buy.

### eSIM Providers Worth Considering

**Airalo** is one of the largest eSIM marketplaces. They offer Korea-specific plans starting around $5 for 1 GB and going up to $15–25 for larger or unlimited plans. The app is straightforward: buy, install, activate.

**Holafly** offers unlimited data plans for Korea. Their pricing is slightly higher ($20–30 for 7 days), but if you want to avoid thinking about data limits entirely, it is a clean option.

**eSIM2Fly by AIS** is a Thailand-based option that covers Korea and most of Asia. It is popular with travelers visiting multiple countries. Plans are competitive, typically $10–15 for 6 GB over 8 days.

All three are legitimate providers with established track records. The right choice depends on how much data you need and whether you want unlimited or pay-per-GB pricing.

## Pocket WiFi — Best for Groups and Families

A pocket WiFi device is a small portable hotspot. You pick it up at Incheon Airport (ICN), carry it with you, and return it before your departure flight.

**How it works:**

1. Reserve online before your trip
2. Pick up the device at the designated counter at ICN (usually in the arrivals hall)
3. Turn it on — it creates a WiFi network that your phone and other devices connect to
4. Return it at ICN before your departure

**Cost:** $5–10/day, so roughly $35–70 for a 7-day trip. This is more expensive than an eSIM per person, but the cost is shared across everyone connecting to it.

**What you get:** Unlimited data (usually throttled after a daily cap, often 500 MB–1 GB of high-speed per day per device equivalent). Connects 5–10 devices simultaneously.

**The catch:** You have to carry an extra device, keep it charged, and remember to return it. If the person carrying the device goes to a different restaurant or gets separated from the group, everyone else loses internet. Battery life is typically 8–12 hours, which may not last a full day of heavy use.

**When it makes sense:** Pocket WiFi is genuinely useful if you are traveling as a family of 4+ and only one person needs navigation. The per-person cost drops below eSIM pricing. For solo travelers or couples, an eSIM is almost always better.

## Local SIM Card — Best If You Need a Korean Phone Number

You can buy a prepaid SIM card at Incheon Airport from KT, SK Telecom, or LG U+ counters. This gives you a Korean phone number along with a data plan.

**How it works:**

1. Find the carrier booth in the ICN arrivals hall (they are clearly signed)
2. Show your passport
3. Choose a plan — typically 5, 10, or 30 days
4. Staff will install the SIM in your phone

**Cost:** $25–50 depending on plan length and data allowance.

**What you get:** Data plus a Korean phone number for calls and SMS. This can be useful if you need to verify accounts on Korean apps (like Kakao T for taxis), receive delivery confirmations, or make local calls.

**The catch:** You need a physical SIM slot (not just eSIM). If your phone uses eSIM only (like newer iPhone models sold in the US), this option will not work. You also lose access to your home number unless your phone supports dual SIM.

**When it makes sense:** Only if you specifically need a Korean phone number. Most first-time visitors do not — messaging apps, Google Maps, and Naver Map all work fine on data-only eSIM plans.

## International Roaming — Not Recommended

Your home carrier likely offers an international roaming plan for Korea. It will work, but it is almost always the most expensive option.

**Typical cost:** $5–15/day depending on your carrier. A 7-day trip could cost $50–105+ just for data.

**What you get:** The convenience of doing nothing — your phone just works when you land. But you pay a significant premium for that convenience, and data caps are often tight (500 MB–1 GB/day).

**When it makes sense:** Almost never. An eSIM gives you the same "just works" experience at a fraction of the cost. The only exception is if your phone does not support eSIM, you do not want a pocket WiFi device, and you prefer to avoid buying a local SIM.

## Our Recommendation

For most first-time visitors to Korea:

1. **Get an eSIM** — cheapest, most convenient, works from the moment you land
2. **Consider pocket WiFi only** if you are a family or group of 4+ who want to share one connection
3. **Skip international roaming** — it is overpriced for what you get
4. **Get a local SIM only** if you specifically need a Korean phone number

Install your eSIM before you board the plane. When you land at ICN and turn off airplane mode, you will have data immediately — which means you can pull up your hotel address, open a map, and figure out the AREX train to Seoul without searching for an airport WiFi network.

## Quick Setup Checklist

Before you fly, make sure you have done the following:

- **Checked eSIM compatibility.** Go to your phone's settings and look for an eSIM or "Add Cellular Plan" option. If it is there, you are good.
- **Purchased and installed the eSIM.** Buy the plan through the provider's app or website, then scan the QR code. Do this while you still have WiFi at home.
- **Set the eSIM as your data line.** On dual-SIM phones, make sure the Korea eSIM is set as your data line and your home SIM handles calls/texts if needed.
- **Turned off data roaming on your home SIM.** This prevents surprise charges from your home carrier.
